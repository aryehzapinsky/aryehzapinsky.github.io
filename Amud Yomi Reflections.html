Reflection on Amud Yomi initiative after finishing our first masechet, Rosh Hashana, and after almost completing review on the masechet. These reflections are meant to reflect on what we have accomplished and direct us for improvements for our next masechet.

1 What is Amud Yomi

1.1 Theory

Amud Yomi is an initiative to bring gemara and gemera parlance into our lives on a daily basis lasting no longer than 20 minutes a session.

1.2 Practice

A minyan of participants who convene to learn an amud of gemara on Monday - Thursday nights, post Maariv. Generally, one member leads and the other participants follow along. The leader varies per day on a mostly voluntary basis.

1.3 Where we've succeeded

  • We have successfully learned through masachet Rosh Hashana on a consistent basis.
  • We have rotated our facilitator to ensure member equality.

1.4 Where there is room to improve

  • There is room to grow to make sure that participants are actively learning material when it is being taught.
  • We have room to work on learning on Friday and Shabbat or Motzash to make the program a truly daily exercise.

2 Initial Learning of the Masechet

2.1 Where we've succeeded

  • We learned Masechet Rosh Hashana.
  • We have a recorded set of sessions covering all amudim.
  • We have had exposure to new halachic concepts (at least some of us).

2.2 Where there is room to improve

  • We should take a more active role in learning each new amud.
  • We should find a better way to review the previous night's material in a succinct manner at the start of each session.

Proposal: The leader of each amud should briefly summarize the amud being taught with 5 bullet points to be quickly reviewed at the beginning of the next night's session. (Possibly in the few minutes prior to starting that night's amud)

3 Review of masechet

3.1 What we've tried

  • We initially tried to review the previous week's amudim on Sunday night. This was not so successful as it is not guarenteed that a person who led a specific amud is present on Sunday of that week.
  • We have tried to review the previous week's amudim in a popcorn style setting on Sunday nights.
  • We have tried to review multiple amudim per night during our Chazara time.

3.2 Where we've succeeded

  • Nightly review of multiple amudim has worked well.
  • Popcorn style Sunday review forces participants to relearn and restruggle with the text.

3.3 Where there is room to improve

  • Chazara should last a shorter amount of time. Too many days were spent reviewing the material, which was re-learning it in a worse manner.
